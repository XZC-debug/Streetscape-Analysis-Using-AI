Why this matters

Walk Score stops at the curb.

The project starts with a simple planning problem: destination access is only half the story. If the crossing is missing, faded, wide, or uncontrolled, the route is not truly walkable even when the neighborhood scores high.

The gap

Existing pedestrian datasets miss crossing-level detail.

Cities usually have either coarse GIS inventories that age out quickly, or expensive field surveys tied to individual projects. There is very little in between.

Our move

A repeatable screening layer.

This workflow uses PASDA aerial imagery and Google Street View to generate usable planning layers that can be refreshed as imagery is updated.

Method

Two computer vision pipelines, one planning interface.

Street View

YOLO detection

Fine-tuned object detection extracts stop signs and traffic lights from intersection-facing imagery.

  • Stop-sign F1: 0.77
  • Traffic-light F1: 0.58
  • Neighborhood-level deployment outputs in GeoJSON and CSV
Aerial imagery

U-Net segmentation

Crosswalk markings are segmented from 15 cm/pixel aerial imagery to produce polygon outputs at scale.

  • Crosswalk F1: 0.94
  • Precision: 0.93 / Recall: 0.95
  • Deployable across multiple Philadelphia study areas
Crossing width

Approach-level measurement

Perpendicular transects estimate curb-to-curb crossing width to create a screening metric planners can use.

  • Intersection-approach estimates in baseline CSV outputs
  • Median width plus spread from sampled transects
  • Designed for prioritization, not engineering survey replacement

Limits and next steps

This is a screening system, not a final audit.

Current limits

Pilot-area training data, no citywide ground-truth audit yet, and lower recall for traffic lights in complex scenes.

Next steps

Expand validation, connect outputs to crash and accessibility datasets, and extend the workflow to more cities and street types.
